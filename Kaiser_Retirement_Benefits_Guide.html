Section 01

The benefit landscape

A retiring Kaiser Permanente employee may draw income from up to three layers. Understanding how they stack helps you frame the whole picture in a first meeting.

🏛️

KP Pension

Employer-funded, defined-benefit lifetime pension under the Kaiser Permanente Retirement Plan (KPRP). Paid as a monthly annuity or a lump sum. The core of this guide.

💼

KP Savings Plans

The Tax-Sheltered Annuity (403(b)) plus, for many groups, an employer-funded Supplemental Savings & Retirement Plan. Held at Vanguard. Investable, portable, inheritable.

🇺🇸

Social Security

The federal layer. Not a KP benefit, but it shapes claiming timing and the income gap you are solving for. Benefit estimates and claiming rules: ssa.gov.

The advisor's lens

The pension and the savings plans play complementary roles. The pension is a guaranteed-floor / longevity asset; the 403(b) and Supplemental Savings accounts are the flexible, growth-and-legacy assets. A common planning move is to use the guaranteed pension income to cover essential expenses, freeing the savings accounts to be invested for growth and used for discretionary spending and inheritance.

Accuracy first

Kaiser Permanente does not run one uniform plan. It runs one master plan (the KPRP) with dozens of region- and union-specific supplements, each with its own pension multiplier, early-retirement terms, and savings-plan features. Figures and tables here are drawn from the SPDs in your project library and are for training. Always verify a client's actual numbers with the Kaiser Permanente Retirement Center (KPRC) and the controlling SPD before giving advice.

Section 02

Eligibility & vesting

Two gates control whether a client has a pension at all: are they an eligible employee, and are they vested?

Who is eligible

Eligibility flows from the client's employee group (salaried/non-union) or their collective bargaining agreement (union). Most groups follow the same pattern: you become a participant on the first anniversary of your hire date if you were compensated for at least 1,000 Hours of Service in the prior 12 months; otherwise on January 1 of the first calendar year you reach 1,000 hours.

Three kinds of “service” (do not mix them up)

The SPDs use three service terms that sound alike but do different jobs. Confusing them is the most common new-advisor error in these plans.

TermWhat countsWhat it controls
Hour of ServiceAny compensated hour, including sick leave, vacation, holidays, and paid leavesThe building block for the other two
Year of ServiceA calendar year (whole or part) with 1,000+ Hours of ServiceParticipation, vesting, and early-retirement eligibility
Credited ServiceA full year at 2,000+ compensated hours; proportional credit below thatThe size of the pension (the formula's third factor)

Note the gap between the two thresholds: a part-time employee working 1,200 hours earns a full Year of Service toward vesting, but only partial Credited Service toward the benefit amount, in the same year.

A quiet win: unused sick leave

In many KP groups, if a vested employee terminates with at least 250 hours of unused sick leave, those hours are converted into additional Credited Service. For a long-tenured client this can measurably raise the pension. Flag it before a client burns down sick leave on the way out the door. A scale check (illustrative): 1,000 unused hours converts to 0.5 years of Credited Service; at a 1.45% multiplier and $6,000 FAMC that is roughly $44/month for life, on the order of $13,000 over a 25-year retirement. Real money, worth protecting, but not a reason to delay an otherwise-right retirement date.

How vesting works

Vesting is when the pension becomes the employee's to keep, even if they leave.

PlanVesting requirement
KP Retirement Plan (defined-benefit pension)5 Years of Service, or reaching age 65 while still employed
Tax-Sheltered Annuity (your own 403(b) money)Immediate — always 100% vested in your own contributions
Supplemental Savings & Retirement Plan (employer money)100% vested in employer contributions (per the Salaried SPD); confirm per group

A client who terminates before vesting in the pension (fewer than 5 Years of Service and under age 65) generally forfeits the pension entirely. Their 403(b) balance is always theirs.

Worked scenario (illustrative): the four-year offer

Priya has 4 years, 3 months of service at Kaiser when a competing hospital calls with a raise. If she leaves now, her pension is forfeited: she vests nothing. If she stays roughly nine more months to cross the 5-year line, then even if she leaves immediately after, a 1.45% multiplier on a $6,000 FAMC locks in about 1.45% × $6,000 × 5 = $435/month for life at 65. The outside offer may still win, but now it has to beat a vested lifetime benefit instead of beating zero. That is the conversation to have before she resigns.

The advisor's lens

A client who is not yet vested and considering leaving Kaiser is making a bigger decision than they may realize — walking away can mean forfeiting the entire pension. If a client is within a year of the 5-year cliff, that timing belongs in the conversation.

A worked example

Worked example — the basic formula

Carolyn retires at age 65 with 20 years of Credited Service. Her Final Average Monthly Compensation is $5,000 and her group's multiplier is 1.5%.
1.5% × $5,000 = $75 per year of service → $75 × 20 = $1,500/month as a Single Life Annuity beginning at age 65.

Now the same numbers, started early: Carolyn's colleague has the identical $1,500 age-65 benefit but begins payments at age 60. Under the representative reduction schedule in Section 05, she receives 85% of it: $1,275/month, for life. She collects five years sooner, but the 15% reduction never goes away. Section 05 covers when that trade makes sense.

Two wrinkles that change the number

1. Blended multipliers. Some contracts changed multipliers over time. For example, one UFCW local uses 1.45% today but calculates pre-November-2009 service at 1.5%. Long-tenured clients can have two rates stitched together.

2. Pension offsets. If a client earned Credited Service under more than one KP plan (or a Joint Labor-Management Trust), the age-65 benefit is offset for the overlapping service so it is not counted twice. Employees who transferred between KP regions or entities, or from Group Health Cooperative in Washington, frequently have an offset. Always check.

Don't confuse these plan types

A few groups do not use the standard multiplier formula: the Colorado Permanente Medical Group (CPMG) physician plan is a cash balance plan (a hypothetical account, not a FAMC formula), and The Permanente Medical Group (TPMG) physicians have their own multi-plan structure. The standalone Stationary Engineers Local 39 pension is a multiemployer (Taft-Hartley) plan, not a KP supplement. When the selector flags one of these, throw out the standard formula and read that plan on its own terms.

Section 04

Final Average Monthly Compensation (FAMC)

FAMC is the “salary” number in the formula, and it is more specific than clients expect. It is not last year's W-2.

Start with the monthly compensation rate

The rate of base pay for the first compensated hour of each month, multiplied by 173.33 (the standard work hours in a month). It is a rate of base pay — not overtime, bonuses, or shift differentials.

Look back over the last 120 months

The plan examines the client's last 10 years of monthly compensation rates.

Take the highest 60 consecutive months

The best consecutive 5-year stretch (usually the most recent) is averaged. Fewer than 60 months of service? It averages all months worked.

Why this matters for advice

Because FAMC is a base-pay rate average, a client's huge final-year of overtime does not inflate the pension — only base-rate raises and promotions do. And because it is the highest 5 years, a client who steps down to a lower-paid or part-time role late in their career usually does not hurt the pension, since the earlier, higher-rate years still count. That can make a phased, part-time glide path into retirement far more attractive than clients assume.

Worked scenario (illustrative): the overtime myth

Rosa is an RN with a $75/hour base rate, so her monthly compensation rate is $75 × 173.33 ≈ $13,000. In her final year she works heavy overtime and grosses $30,000 above base pay. Effect on her FAMC: zero. FAMC is built from the base-pay rate, not from W-2 earnings. A $2/hour base-rate raise, by contrast, lifts the monthly rate by about $347 and flows straight into the highest-60-month average. When a client asks “should I load up on overtime before I retire,” the honest answer is that it helps their savings, not their pension.

Worked scenario (illustrative): the phase-down that costs less than feared

Vic, 62, wants to drop to a 60% schedule for his last three years. His hourly base rate does not change, so his monthly compensation rate, and therefore his FAMC, does not change either. What slows is Credited Service: at roughly 1,200 compensated hours a year he accrues about 0.6 years annually instead of 1.0, so three phased years cost him about 1.2 years of Credited Service versus staying full-time. His FAMC stays anchored to his highest 5-year stretch. For many clients that trade, a modestly smaller pension in exchange for three easier years, is far better than they assumed when they thought part-time would “wreck the average.”

The IRS caps the annual pay that can be counted toward FAMC and caps the total annual pension a qualified plan can pay; both limits are indexed each year. High earners and physicians can hit these ceilings, so confirm the current-year limits with the KPRC rather than quoting a remembered figure. The official dollar limits are published at irs.gov: COLA limits on benefits and contributions.

This is where advisors most often catch costly client misconceptions. When a client can start the pension — and how much of it is cut for starting early — turns on age and Years of Service.

Who gets this schedule (and who doesn't)

The table above is the subsidized early-retirement schedule, and it generally belongs to employees who qualify for Early Retirement and retire from active Kaiser employment. A client who terminated years earlier with a Deferred Vested pension typically faces a steeper, actuarial reduction for starting before 65, or may need to wait until 65 entirely. Quoting the subsidized table to a former employee can overstate their benefit by a wide margin. Before quoting any early-start number, confirm with the KPRC which reduction basis applies to this client's status.

Scenario (illustrative): the fork in the road. Ana and Ben are both 54 with 14 years of service. Ana resigns today: she is vested, but she never qualified for Early Retirement, so her pension is a Deferred Vested benefit that generally waits until 65 (or takes a steeper actuarial cut where an earlier start is allowed at all). Ben stays 18 more months, reaches age 55 with 15 years, and retires from active status: he qualifies for Early Retirement and the subsidized schedule, and could start at 56 at roughly 65% of his age-65 benefit. Nearly identical careers; the 18 months changed which reduction table applies for the rest of their lives.

The trap: the reduction is steep, and permanent

Starting the pension at 55 instead of 65 can cut it by 40% — for life. Many clients see “I'm eligible to retire at 55” and hear “I get my full pension at 55.” Those are very different statements. Eligibility to start is not the same as an unreduced benefit.

The Rule of 75 is a sum, but read the fine print

Unlike some employers' rigid age/service pairs, Kaiser's Early Retirement gate genuinely is age + Years of Service ≥ 75 (e.g., 55 + 20, 58 + 17, 60 + 15). Meeting it opens the door to start early — but the early-commencement reduction still applies for every year before 65 unless the client waits. The Rule of 75 controls eligibility to start early; the age table controls how much is cut.

Worked scenario (illustrative): what one more year buys

Nadia is 59 with 24 years of Credited Service, a $7,000 FAMC, and a 1.45% multiplier.
Start now: 1.45% × $7,000 × 24 = $2,436, reduced to 80% at age 59 = $1,949/month.
Work one more year: 25 years of service and an 85% factor at 60 = $2,537.50 × 85% = $2,157/month.
One more year of work bought a roughly 11% permanent raise, before counting any FAMC growth from raises in that year. This is the calculation to run, in both directions, whenever a client is within a year or two of a milestone. Sometimes the answer is that the extra year is not worth it; the point is that the client should see the number before deciding.

The advisor's lens

When a client is close to a milestone — the 5-year vesting cliff, the 15th year of service, or an age where the reduction eases sharply — a few more months of work can be worth tens of thousands in lifetime income. Mapping the client's exact age and service against these rules is one of the highest-leverage things you do. Use the estimator next to put dollars on it.

Section 07

How the pension gets paid

At retirement the client chooses a form of payment. The choice is permanent — once payments begin it cannot be changed — so this is a decision to get right the first time.

Single Life Annuity

Covers one lifetime
  • Highest monthly payment
  • Standard form if unmarried
  • Stops at death — nothing for a survivor
  • No inflation adjustment
  • Taxable as ordinary income

Joint & Survivor (50 / 66⅔ / 75%)

Covers two lifetimes
  • Continues a % to spouse/partner after death
  • 50% J&S is the default if married
  • Lower monthly payment than single life
  • Beneficiary is locked once payments begin
  • No inflation adjustment
  • Taxable as ordinary income

Lump Sum

One-time payout
  • Rollable to a traditional or Roth IRA
  • Investable & inheritable
  • Longevity & market risk shift to the client
  • Amount moves with interest rates (see below)
  • RMDs apply later to pre-tax rollovers (Roth is exempt)
  • Direct payout triggers 20% withholding

Interest rates move the lump sum

The lump sum is not a fixed pot of money waiting at the plan. It is the actuarial present value of the client's annuity, converted to a single payment using IRS-prescribed corporate-bond interest rates (the “segment rates,” published monthly by the IRS) that the plan resets on a set schedule. The relationship is inverse: when the conversion rates rise, the lump sum shrinks; when rates fall, it grows. The monthly annuity is untouched by any of this; only the lump-sum conversion moves.

The effect is not small. Because the lump sum discounts decades of future payments, even a one-percentage-point move in rates can change a large lump sum by tens of thousands of dollars. Plans typically fix the rates using a lookback month and hold them for a stability period (often the plan year), which means two clients with identical pensions can receive meaningfully different lump sums simply because they commenced on opposite sides of a rate reset.

How to use this with a client

When a client is leaning toward the lump sum, timing becomes part of the advice. Three concrete moves: (1) ask the KPRC which rates apply to the client's intended commencement date and when the plan next resets them; (2) if a reset is approaching and rates have moved since the current basis was set, request benefit quotes for commencement dates on each side of the reset and compare them in dollars; (3) put the result in context. A rate-driven difference can justify shifting a start date by a few months, but it should not override the bigger suitability question of annuity versus lump sum. Confirm the plan's specific rate basis and reset schedule with the KPRC; do not estimate it.

The commencement-timing trap

A client who says “I'll just wait a bit longer” may assume the lump sum only grows with time. If conversion rates have risen in the meantime, waiting past a rate reset can shrink the quoted lump sum even as the underlying annuity accrues. When a client is near a plan-rate reset, get quotes before assuming which direction time is working.

Defaults & spousal consent

If the client is married, federal law makes the 50% Joint & Survivor annuity the default, and the spouse is entitled to it. Electing anything else — single life, a different J&S percentage, or a lump sum — requires written, notarized spousal consent (generally within 90 days before payments begin). If unmarried, the default is the Single Life Annuity.

The advisor's lens: annuity vs. lump sum

This is the marquee pension decision. The annuity is a longevity hedge with a guaranteed floor and no market risk; the lump sum offers control, growth potential, flexibility, and a legacy asset but transfers investment and longevity risk to the client. Weigh the client's health and family longevity, other guaranteed income, the survivor's own resources, legacy goals, and the annuity's implied payout rate versus what a diversified portfolio could reasonably support. There is no universal right answer — but there is a right answer for this client.

Three clients, three different right answers

Illustrative composites showing how the same menu of options resolves differently. In each case, note which factor the decision turned on.

Elena, 64 — the couple with one pension

Married; husband, 66, has Social Security but no pension of his own

Elena is healthy with strong family longevity. Her pension is the household's only guaranteed income beyond Social Security, and if she dies first her husband loses her paycheck-replacement entirely. They kept a Joint & Survivor annuity and compared the 50/66⅔/75% levels against the cost of life insurance. Deciding factor: the survivor's own resources, or lack of them.

Devon, 65 — the single client with legacy goals

Single; substantial 403(b); adult children; essentials already covered

Devon's Social Security and other income cover his baseline spending, and an annuity would stop at his death with nothing for his kids. He took the lump sum as a direct rollover to an IRA, accepting market risk he could afford, in exchange for growth potential and an inheritable asset. Deciding factor: the annuity's value dies with him; the goal was legacy.

Sam, 62 — the client with a health diagnosis

Married; serious health condition; spouse has her own pension

A lifetime annuity is a longevity hedge, and Sam's longevity outlook is impaired while his spouse is already protected by her own pension. The team examined the lump sum and, separately, a Single Life Annuity (with notarized spousal consent) paired with existing life insurance. Deciding factor: a longevity hedge is worth less when longevity is the known risk, and the survivor was already covered.

Composite teaching examples, not client records. The right structure depends on the client's full picture; document the reasoning either way.

Tax mechanic to know

A lump sum paid directly to the client triggers a mandatory 20% federal withholding. A direct rollover to an IRA or other eligible plan avoids withholding and preserves tax deferral — usually the cleaner path when a lump sum is chosen. The rollover rules, including the withholding mechanics, are at irs.gov: rollovers of retirement plan distributions.

Section 08 · The Savings Plans

The TSA 403(b) & Supplemental Savings

The defined-contribution side, recordkept by Vanguard. There are two moving parts: the money the employee puts in (the TSA) and, for many groups, money the employer puts in (the Supplemental Savings & Retirement Plan).

Tax-Sheltered Annuity (TSA) — a 403(b)

The employee's own savings plan. Eligible from hire, regardless of schedule. New hires are auto-enrolled at 2% pre-tax, auto-escalating 1%/year up to 6%, invested in the plan's default (QDIA) unless the client chooses otherwise. Contributions can run 1–75% of pay up to the IRS 403(b) contribution limit (indexed yearly), pre-tax and/or Roth.

Supplemental Savings & Retirement Plan (SSRP)

Employer-funded. Where offered (e.g., the Salaried group), Kaiser automatically contributes a set percentage of eligible pay (5% in the Salaried SPD) with no employee contribution required, and the employee is 100% vested. This is free retirement money layered on top of the pension.

Confirm the specifics per group

Whether a group gets the SSRP, the exact employer percentage, any matching, Roth availability, and the investment lineup are governed by the savings-plan SPD and the current collective bargaining agreement. Some union groups instead participate in a jointly-administered trust. Confirm each client's plan in their Vanguard account before building a strategy.

The 45-day and 90-day windows

Auto-enrollment is reversible, briefly. A newly enrolled employee has 45 days from hire to opt out (or change the rate, elect Roth, or opt out of auto-escalation), and 90 days from the first deduction to cancel and have auto-enrollment contributions refunded. After that, participation continues until the client changes it.

Pre-tax vs. Roth: a career-stage decision

Whether a client should direct 403(b) contributions to pre-tax or Roth turns largely on their tax rate now versus their expected tax rate when the money comes out. Career stage is usually the best proxy.

Early career / lower-earning years → lean Roth

A younger or lower-paid employee is often in a low bracket, so a pre-tax deduction is worth little. Paying tax now at a low rate and letting decades of growth come out tax-free is frequently the stronger trade. Roth dollars also do not add to MAGI in retirement, which helps with future IRMAA and Social Security taxation.

Peak-earning years → lean pre-tax

A senior employee at the top of the scale may be in a high bracket where the pre-tax deduction is genuinely valuable. Deferring now and drawing later — potentially in lower-income early-retirement years before the pension, Social Security, and RMDs stack up — can lower the lifetime tax bill.

The Roth 403(b) has its own rules: qualified tax-free withdrawals require the account to be 5+ years old and the client to be 59½ (or disabled/deceased). In-plan Roth conversions are available but create taxable income in the conversion year — and the tax must be paid from outside the plan. The IRS maintains the full designated-Roth rule set at irs.gov: designated Roth accounts.

The advisor's lens

An advisor can manage these Vanguard-held accounts and advise on allocation. Before optimizing anything fancier, make sure the basics are working: the client is capturing any employer contribution, contributing at a rate that fits their goals (not just the 6% auto-escalation ceiling), and invested appropriately rather than sitting in the default fund by inertia. The 403(b) is where ongoing investment management lives — the natural complement to the pension's guaranteed income.

Section 09 · Advisor Toolkit

Retiree medical & other post-employment benefits

For a client retiring before 65, health coverage is often the single biggest financial question — bigger than the pension math. Several KP SPDs describe post-employment benefits that sit alongside the pension.

Modified Retiree Medical Benefit

Many groups describe a retiree medical benefit with its own age-and-service eligibility rules, separate from the pension. Availability, cost-sharing, and whether any subsidy applies vary widely by region and contract, and have been reduced for newer hires. Confirm each client's eligibility in the governing health-and-welfare SPD.

Retiree dental & life insurance

Some groups extend retiree dental coverage and a reduced retiree life insurance benefit. These are modest but real — worth confirming so a client does not double-buy coverage they already have.

Why the pre-65 gap matters most

A client who retires before age 65 is not yet Medicare-eligible. Individual coverage in that gap can run well over $1,000–$2,000/month per person. Even unsubsidized access to a group retiree plan can beat the open market, and where a subsidy still applies it is worth materially more. A client who leaves a few months short of a retiree-medical eligibility milestone can lose access entirely — often a six-figure decision. Map it before they give notice. For gap-year pricing comparisons, marketplace plans and subsidies are at HealthCare.gov; Medicare eligibility and costs are at medicare.gov.

Verify: don't assume

Retiree medical eligibility and subsidies are governed by the separate health-and-welfare SPD and the current bargaining agreement, not the pension section, and they change over time. Confirm the client's specific situation before promising anything.

Section 10

IRMAA & Roth planning

Coordinating pension & savings income with IRMAA in mind

Once a client reaches Medicare, their premiums are no longer flat. The Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount (IRMAA) adds a surcharge to Medicare Part B and Part D premiums when modified adjusted gross income (MAGI) crosses a threshold. Pension income, 403(b) withdrawals, and Roth conversions all feed MAGI, so how a client sequences distributions directly drives what they pay for Medicare.

FeatureWhat it means for planning
Two-year lookbackThis year's premium is set by MAGI from two years ago. A distribution or conversion today shows up in premiums ~2 years later — plan the income, then watch the downstream year.
Cliff, not a rampCrossing a threshold by even $1 triggers the full surcharge for that tier — no phase-in. The dollar that puts a client over can carry a very high effective marginal cost.
Per personFor a married couple, both Medicare-enrolled spouses pay the surcharge once joint MAGI crosses a tier.

Because the thresholds and surcharge amounts change yearly, always pull the current figures rather than a remembered number: medicare.gov: Medicare costs & IRMAA · ssa.gov: how income affects your Medicare premiums (SSA determines the surcharge and publishes the current tier tables).

Roth conversions with brackets and IRMAA in view

Converting pre-tax dollars to Roth means voluntarily recognizing income now to buy tax-free growth and future MAGI control. The window that often makes this attractive for KP clients is the gap between retiring and the onset of Social Security and required minimum distributions — a stretch of unusually low taxable income, especially for someone who retired early.

How to size a conversion

The disciplined approach is to “fill the bracket”: convert just enough to use up a low or moderate tax bracket without spilling into the next one — and, once the client is within two years of Medicare or already enrolled, without crossing the next IRMAA tier. Model the conversion's MAGI impact two years forward for the IRMAA effect, not just the current-year tax.

Scenario (illustrative): the December conversion

A retired couple, both enrolled in Medicare, does a year-end Roth conversion without checking the tier tables and lands $400 over an IRMAA threshold. Two years later, both spouses pay that tier's full Part B and Part D surcharge for twelve months. The income tax on the extra $400 was trivial; the downstream premium cost was hundreds of times larger. The lesson: near year-end, model MAGI to the dollar against the current tier table (linked above) before converting, and leave a buffer for surprise income such as late capital-gain distributions.

Not advice: a framework

Conversion and contribution strategy depend on the individual's full tax picture, state taxes, legacy goals, and assumptions about future rates. Use these as factors to evaluate with the client and their tax professional, not as a default recommendation. Advisors do not provide tax advice.

Section 11

Strategy playbook

The recurring, high-value decisions where a Kaiser client benefits most from advice.

Identify the exact plan

Use the left-rail selector to pin down the client's group, region, and multiplier. Confirm it against their SPD — and flag the exceptions (CPMG cash balance, TPMG physician plans, Local 39 multiemployer).

Map age & service against the milestones

Check vesting (5 yrs), Early Retirement (55 + 15, or Rule of 75), and the ages where the reduction eases. Identify the nearest milestone and what reaching it is worth.

Reconstruct the pension

Multiplier × FAMC × Credited Service. Watch for blended multipliers, pension offsets from prior KP service, and unused-sick-leave credit. Verify with the KPRC.

Run annuity vs. lump sum

Compare the guaranteed annuity against a prudent withdrawal from the rolled-over lump sum, factoring health, longevity, spouse, and legacy. Document the reasoning.

Choose the survivor structure deliberately

Single life vs. 50/66⅔/75% J&S is permanent and needs spousal consent. Align it with life insurance and the survivor's own income.

Solve the pre-65 healthcare gap

Confirm retiree-medical eligibility before the client gives notice. This is frequently the highest-stakes number in the whole plan.

Optimize the Vanguard accounts

Confirm employer contributions are captured, set a contribution rate that fits the goal (not just the auto-escalation ceiling), match pre-tax/Roth to the client's bracket, and fix default-fund inertia.

Sequence income & manage MAGI

Coordinate pension start date, Social Security claiming, and 403(b) withdrawals. Size withdrawals and Roth conversions to fill the bracket without crossing the next IRMAA tier, projecting MAGI two years forward.

Case study: the playbook applied

An illustrative composite; figures use this guide's representative values. Open in a separate window

Denise is 58, an RN in the California Nurses Association group in Northern California, with 22 years of Credited Service and an $8,200 FAMC. She is married; her husband is 60 and has Social Security ahead but no pension of his own. She wants to retire “as soon as it works.” Work each step below: a correct answer unlocks the next, and the full case summary appears when all eight are done. Wrong answers get a hint, so use the guide's sections and the left-rail selector as you go.

Step 1Identify the plan

Use the plan selector in the left rail to find the pension multiplier for Denise's group (California Nurses Association, Nor Cal). Enter it as a percentage.

1.45%. NorCal CNA uses the standard defined-benefit formula with a 1.45% multiplier. With a real client, confirm it against her SPD before quoting anything.

Step 2Map the milestones

Denise is 58 with 22 Years of Service, retiring from active employment. Using the representative schedule in Section 05, what percentage of her age-65 benefit would she receive if payments start now?

75%. She qualifies for Early Retirement two ways: age 55 with 15+ years, and the Rule of 75 (58 + 22 = 80). She can start now, but at 75% of the age-65 amount, permanently.

Step 3Reconstruct the pension

Compute her benefit (FAMC $8,200; 22 years of Credited Service). (a) The unreduced monthly Single Life amount at 65 = multiplier × FAMC × Credited Service. (b) The monthly amount if she starts now at 58.

$2,615.80 at 65; $1,961.85 at 58. 1.45% × $8,200 × 22 = $2,615.80/mo, reduced to 75% for starting at 58. Show her the alternative too: working to 60 (24 years, 85% factor) pays about $2,425.56/mo. Two more years of work are worth roughly +$464/mo for life. That comparison is the advice.

Step 4Annuity vs lump sum

Market rates have risen since the plan's current lump-sum conversion basis was set, and the plan resets its segment rates soon. If Denise waits until after the reset for her lump-sum quote, the quote will most likely be:

Smaller. The lump sum is the present value of her annuity discounted at the segment rates: higher rates, smaller lump sum. The move is to get KPRC quotes for commencement dates on each side of the reset and compare them in dollars. Her monthly annuity is unaffected either way.

Step 5Survivor structure

Denise is married and her husband has no pension of his own. Which payment form is her legal default, and what would electing anything else require?

The 50% J&S is the federal default for married participants, and her husband is entitled to it; anything else takes his written, notarized consent, generally within 90 days before payments begin. Because he has no pension of his own, they compared the higher J&S levels against life-insurance alternatives before deciding.

Step 6Pre-65 healthcare

If Denise retires now at 58, how many years must her health coverage bridge before Medicare eligibility?

Seven years. That gap, not the pension math, was the number that nearly moved her date. Confirm retiree-medical eligibility in the H&W SPD and benchmark marketplace pricing before she gives notice.

Step 7Savings accounts

What are the first moves on her Vanguard 403(b)?

Basics first. Capture any employer money per her CBA, set a deliberate allocation, and only then consider anything fancier. The 403(b) is the flexible, growth-and-legacy layer alongside the pension's guaranteed floor.

Step 8Sequence & MAGI

Her plan tapers Roth conversions as she approaches age 63. Why?

The two-year lookback. Her gap years between retirement and Social Security/RMDs are ideal for bracket-fill conversions, but income at 63 becomes her premium at 65. Size each conversion against the current IRMAA tier table (Section 10) before year-end.

Section 12

Protections & rules to know

PBGC insurance

The defined-benefit pension is federally insured by the Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation up to legal limits. Taking a lump sum forfeits this guarantee. Current guarantee tables by age: pbgc.gov: maximum guarantee.

QDRO (divorce)

A Qualified Domestic Relations Order can assign part of the pension or savings account to a former spouse. Benefits otherwise cannot be assigned, pledged, or reached by creditors. The Department of Labor's guide to QDRO rules: dol.gov: QDROs.

Spousal rights in the savings plan

Under the savings plans a spouse is generally entitled to 50% of the account at death unless a notarized waiver is on file. Special rules apply to married participants under age 35. Beneficiary designations live on Vanguard's system.

Pension offsets / no double credit

Service and pay cannot count twice across KP plans. Clients who moved between KP regions, entities, or from Group Health Cooperative may see an offset that reduces the new-plan benefit for overlapping service.

Required minimum distributions

Postponed pensions and savings accounts must generally begin by the federal RMD deadline. Coordinate RMDs with the client's broader tax and IRMAA plan. Current ages, deadlines, and rules: irs.gov: RMD FAQs.

Rehire & transfer rules

Rehired employees and inter-regional transfers are governed by the national Inter-Regional Transfer policy. Prior service may or may not count — always review the relevant plan document.

Glossary

KPRP
Kaiser Permanente Retirement Plan — the master, qualified defined-benefit pension plan, delivered through many region- and union-specific supplements.
Plan multiplier
The percentage (1.2%–1.5% by group) applied to FAMC and Credited Service in the pension formula.
Final Average Monthly Compensation (FAMC)
The average of the highest 60 consecutive months (of the last 120) of the monthly base-pay rate (hourly base × 173.33).
Credited Service
Service that sizes the benefit: a full year at 2,000+ compensated hours, proportional below that; can include unused-sick-leave credit.
Year of Service
A calendar year with 1,000+ Hours of Service; used for participation, vesting, and early-retirement eligibility.
Hour of Service
Any compensated hour, including sick leave, vacation, holidays, and paid leave.
Vesting
The point the pension is the employee's to keep — 5 Years of Service (or age 65 while employed). TSA employee money is always vested.
Normal Retirement
Age 65 — the pension is payable unreduced.
Early Retirement
Age 55 with 15 Years of Service, or age + service ≥ 75 (“Rule of 75”); benefit reduced if started before 65.
Deferred Vested Pension
The benefit for a vested employee who leaves before qualifying for Early/Normal Retirement; generally starts at 65. Where an earlier start is allowed, the reduction is typically actuarial and steeper than the subsidized early-retirement schedule.
Single Life Annuity
The base form of payment — a monthly benefit for the retiree's lifetime, with nothing continuing to a survivor.
Joint & Survivor (J&S)
An annuity that continues 50%, 66⅔%, or 75% to a survivor. The 50% J&S is the default for married participants.
Pension Offset
A reduction that prevents the same service from being counted under two KP plans (or a Group Health / trust plan).
Segment rates
IRS-prescribed corporate-bond interest rates used to convert an annuity into a lump sum. Higher rates produce smaller lump sums, and vice versa; the plan resets them on a set schedule. Confirm the applicable rates and reset timing with the KPRC.
TSA
Tax-Sheltered Annuity — Kaiser's 403(b) employee savings plan at Vanguard; auto-enroll 2% escalating to 6%; pre-tax and/or Roth.
SSRP
Supplemental Savings & Retirement Plan — employer-funded defined-contribution account (e.g., 5% of pay in the Salaried SPD); 100% vested.
Cash Balance Plan
An account-style defined-benefit plan (used by CPMG physicians) — a hypothetical balance rather than a FAMC formula.
KPRC
Kaiser Permanente Retirement Center — the pension administrator; the authoritative source for a client's actual pension figures.
PBGC
Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation — federal insurer of the defined-benefit pension.
QDRO
Qualified Domestic Relations Order — a court order splitting a benefit in divorce.
SPD
Summary Plan Description — the controlling plain-language plan document; it governs in any conflict with this guide.
IRMAA
Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount — an income-based surcharge on Medicare Part B and D premiums, set by MAGI from two years prior.
MAGI
Modified adjusted gross income — drives IRMAA tiers; fed by pension income, 403(b) withdrawals, and Roth conversions.
